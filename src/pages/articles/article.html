Coronavirus and the Aerospace Industry: Approaching Problems & Solutions

Rob Pakalski Mar 6, 2021 | 3 min read

Coronavirus has already negatively impacted the global economy and Harvard Business Review predicts the worst effects are still to come in mid-March. Industries that heavily rely on Chinese manufacturing are the most vulnerable; think Apple, Nike, and Proctor & Gamble which, alone, has a reported 387 suppliers in China.

Due to ITAR and DFAR restrictions, most aerospace companies do not work directly with Chinese suppliers. But industry leaders should still be concerned. The aerospace market has already suffered, and our research predicts more setbacks are likely to come.

Coronavirus will serve as a test for supply-base risk management effectiveness. Any aerospace organization unable to detect their risk exposure will experience trouble.

Affected Areas in the Industry

Airbus completion centers for its A320 and A330 models in Tianjin (outside of Beijing) were forced to close for a period in February. The facility employs 500 people and accounts for 10% of the A320neos global production, with six aircrafts being built per month.

Boeing has three subsidiaries, four joint ventures, and more than 35 direct suppliers in China. In February Boeing leadership said that they are “working through plans to delay office openings, provide masks, offer informational briefings and facilitate work from home options when available.” With the 737 Max being grounded, Boeing will be much less affected by the virus since most production is on hold.

Then there are many global conventions getting canceled or delayed such as JEC World, a premier trade-show for the composite industry, which is postponed to mid-March.

These instances may seem minor or unrelatable to the non-giants in the industry, but indirect challenges are likely to emerge and impact aerospace companies of all sizes.

The Approaching Problem for all Aerospace Players

Most aerospace companies across the globe do not understand their risk exposure in Asia. That is because few know the locations of all companies providing parts to their direct Tier 1 suppliers. A critical Tier 1 supplier of yours may be located in Oklahoma, but many of the required sub-components – perhaps a sensor - might be coming from China (or other areas of concern).

An analysis by Dun & Bradstreet revealed that 50,000 companies have Tier 1 suppliers in affected regions of china, but a further 5 million have Tier 2 suppliers in that region. This data indicates the likelihood of your direct suppliers relying on Chinese vendors, raising a serious risk of supply shortages for certain sub-components.

What’s the Solution?

It is unrealistic and inefficient to reach out to every one of your Tier 1 suppliers to ask if they are sourcing sub-components from any Tier 2 suppliers located in COVID-19 affected areas such as China. The first step should be determining which vendors in your supply base are critical (aka Strategic Suppliers).

Supply chain leaders often make the mistake of categorizing a supplier as Strategic based on spend: suppliers you spend the most with are labeled “Strategic.” But we believe this to be a common error as many low-spend suppliers providing commoditized items represent an immense risk. Instead of labeling by spend, use Time To Recovery (TTR).

Harvard Business Review defines TTR as the time it would take for a particular node (such as a supplier facility, a distribution center, or a transportation hub) to be restored to full functionality after a disruption. Strategic suppliers can be identified based on high TTR scores.

Other criteria may be used to label a supplier “Strategic,” but regardless of the criteria, aerospace supply managers need to have conversations with these suppliers immediately. The team should walk away from those meetings with a clear understanding of whether any sub-components are being sourced in China.

If you discover that your Strategic Tier 1 supplier does require a supply base from China, a strategy must be developed immediately. Ask if an alternative supplier in a non-affected area can be located, and, if so, help your Tier 1 develop a dual-source strategy for the component. If you want to play it extra safe, which we recommend given the unpredictability of this virus, provide advance payment to your Tier 1 supplier so they are able to stock up on inventory of the sub-component.

Why This Situation Is Unique

Preparing for low-probability, high-impact events is very difficult for supply managers. Hopefully, we wake up tomorrow and a coronavirus vaccine is developed, which is certainly a possibility. But relying on this to occur and not acting now could end up being costly.
